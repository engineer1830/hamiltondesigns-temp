# Refiner’s Fire

Silver Refining (Cupellation)

Of all the noble metals, silver is the best at conducting heat and electricity (according to the Jefferson National Linear Accelerator Labratory) and is also the best at reflecting visible light.

Cupellation is a refining process in metallurgy where ores are treated with high temperatures to liquify metal and separate noble metals (ie. Gold and silver) from base metals such as lead, copper, zinc, arsenic, etc. Noble metals do not react as base metals do. Therefore, when the temperatures are hot enough, the metals liquify and base metals react (oxidize) and form slags and other compounds that are separated from the unreacted noble metals. When a porous bone ash cupel is coated with reactants (such as lead foil) and the silver is heated such that the impurities react with the oxygen in the atmosphere and lead lining, the silver is refined or purified to the point that it is bright and shiney.

Silver melts at 960 C. When a metal, including silver is overheated, it will change not only the appearance, but also the physical and electrical properties negatively. Typical metal properties include:

* Ductility — ability to be drawn (stretched) without fracture (such as a wire)
* Malleability — strong, but easily bent and shaped
* Tensile strength — resistance to breaking under tension

When a metal is overheated through too much refining, these properties change (sometimes permanently).
