# Discipleship

Exactly when did we think that discipleship was casual and comfortable?  Why do we expect others around us to be perfect while kindly overlooking our short comings?  Discipleship is the path that we undertake to learn how to forgive others as we also hope to be forgiven, not only by others but by God Himself. The path of discipleship is no less than learning and living the Doctrine of Christ and the many varied layers of life that the Atonement heals. Discipleship is not a quick journey. Rather, it is a lifetime pursuit that walks the Covenant Path to prepare ourselves to return to our Home with our Heavenly Father and Family. 

Discipleship is recognizing that our path will be marked with periods of challenge and trials and that these trials, whether the product of our [poor?] choices or not related to choices at all. Learning and recognizing that trials are exactly what is required to help us change and shed the rougher edges requires faith - faith in a plan we lack the ability to see afar or even close at times. 

Elder Maxwell says “one’s life, therefore, cannot be both faith-filled and stress-free . . . How can you and I really expect to glide naively through life, as if to say, ‘Lord, give me experience, but not grief, not sorrow, not pain, not opposition, not betrayal, and certainly not to be forsaken. Keep from me, Lord, all those experiences which made Thee what Thou art!  Then let me come and dwell with Thee and fully share Thy joy!”

In his October 1995 conference talk, Elder Maxwell taught:  To the extent that we are not willing to be led by the Lord, we will be driven by our appetites, or we will be greatly preoccupied with the lesser things of the day. The remedy is implicit in the marvelous lamentation of King Benjamin: “For how knoweth a man the master whom he has not served, and who is a stranger unto him, and is far from the thoughts and intents of his heart?” (Mosiah 5:13). For many moderns, sad to say, the query “What think ye of Christ?” (Matt. 22:42) would be answered, “I really don’t think of Him at all!”

I have learned through my personal experiences that if the trials of our lives do not push us to the very edge of our faith, we will not experience the the required temperature to purify us. It is literally in that moment of being on the edge and limits of our faith that we come to understand that HIS will must become our will for our faith to be sufficient. It is in those moments that we will learn that the merits and reality of the Atonement has been supporting us constantly.  It is at this moment that we embrace that we must completely rely upon our Savior that our faith will be sufficient to trust that HE knows perfectly the temperature required for us to be purified and refined. 

There are strong headwinds that attempt to blow us off of our path of discipleship. 

Elder Holland (https://www.lds.org/general-conference/2014/04/the-cost-and-blessings-of-discipleship?lang=eng)

Sadly enough, my young friends, it is a characteristic of our age that if people want any gods at all, they want them to be gods who do not demand much, comfortable gods, smooth gods who not only don’t rock the boat but don’t even row it, gods who pat us on the head, make us giggle, then tell us to run along and pick marigolds.

Be strong. Live the gospel faithfully even if others around you don’t live it at all. Defend your beliefs with courtesy and with compassion, but defend them. 

(https://www.lds.org/general-conference/2012/04/the-laborers-in-the-vineyard?lang=eng)

This parable — like all parables is not really about laborers or wages any more than the others are about sheep and goats. This is a story about God’s goodness, His patience and forgiveness, and the Atonement of the Lord Jesus Christ. It is a story about generosity and compassion. It is a story about grace. It underscores the thought I heard many years ago that surely the thing God enjoys most about being God is the thrill of being merciful, especially to those who don’t expect it and often feel they don’t deserve it.

I especially make an appeal for husbands and fathers, priesthood bearers or prospective priesthood bearers, to, as Lehi said, “Awake! and arise from the dust … and be men.”5 Not always but often it is the men who choose not to answer the call to “come join the ranks.”6 Women and children frequently seem more willing. Brethren, step up. Do it for your sake. Do it for the sake of those who love you and are praying that you will respond. Do it for the sake of the Lord Jesus Christ, who paid an unfathomable price for the future He wants you to have.
